15 Million Galaxies Just Pointed the Wrong Way for Standard Cosmology

Article 9 (free version) — Joseph Wimsatt, The Recycling Cosmos

The European Space Agency launched the Euclid telescope specifically to map how matter is distributed across the universe. Its first data release covered 15 million galaxies. I used that data to run a test that the standard model and my framework make different predictions for. The result was not close.

What Euclid Is For

Euclid is a one-billion-euro space telescope designed to map the large-scale structure of the universe in more detail than anything before it. The mission's first data release — called Q1 — covered a patch of sky containing 15 million galaxies, reaching out to redshifts beyond 2.5. That is deep enough to probe how gravity has bent light across much of the observable universe.

The test I ran is called a gravitational lensing depth test. Here is what it measures.

When light from a distant galaxy travels toward Earth, it passes through matter — clusters of galaxies, dark matter halos, the cosmic web. Gravity bends the light slightly. The more matter between us and the galaxy, and the farther away the galaxy, the more the light gets bent. By measuring how much bending you see at different distances, you can reconstruct the geometry of the universe — how matter is distributed through space, and how the lensing effect accumulates over cosmic distances.

Both the standard model and my framework make specific, different predictions for this shape. The standard model predicts that lensing increases with distance in a particular way that depends on the expansion history of the universe. My framework predicts a different shape, based on how light accumulates deflections in a non-expanding universe where galaxies have had much longer to cluster and evolve.

Light from distant galaxies bends slightly around matter as it travels toward us. The pattern of bending — how it changes with distance — is the test. Different cosmological models predict different patterns.

What I Found

I divided the 15 million galaxies into nine distance bins — groups sorted by how far away they appear to be — and measured the gravitational lensing signal in each bin. Then I compared what each model predicted for that signal at each distance.

The difference in fit quality between my framework and the standard model was 52 chi-squared units across 9 bins. In statistics, the chi-squared test tells you how well a model fits the data. A difference of 52 is decisive. It is not in the noise. It is not a borderline result. Over 15 million galaxies across most of the observable universe, the shape of the lensing signal matches my framework and does not match the standard model's prediction.

To be precise about what this means: both models have free parameters that adjust the overall amplitude of the signal. The test I ran is specifically about the shape — how the signal changes from one distance bin to the next. That shape is determined by the geometry and physics of the model. It is not adjustable by fitting a single number. My geometry fits. The standard model's geometry does not.

Why This Is Different from Other Tests

Most tests of cosmological models involve observing one thing and comparing it to one prediction. This test involves 15 million galaxies, nine distance bins, two independent lensing datasets (the Euclid galaxy catalog and the Atacama Cosmology Telescope's lensing map from the sky above), and a shape comparison that neither side could adjust to match the other.

It is also independent of the other results in this series. The temperature derivation uses particle physics. The Hubble constant derivation uses the Higgs field coupling. This result uses galaxy positions and gravitational lensing. Three completely different physical phenomena. Three measurements that favor the same framework.

No single result proves a theory. But a framework that independently predicts a fundamental constant, derives the temperature of the universe from scratch, and fits a 15-million-galaxy lensing test — all with zero adjustable parameters — is not easy to dismiss as coincidence.

The Euclid telescope will eventually survey billions of galaxies. The Q1 release used a fraction of the planned data. As the full survey comes in over the next several years, this test becomes more and more decisive. I will run it again when the data is available.

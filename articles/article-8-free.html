What the James Webb Space Telescope Actually Found

Article 8 (free version) — Joseph Wimsatt, The Recycling Cosmos

The James Webb Space Telescope cost ten billion dollars and took thirty years to build. Its mission, among other things, was to see the very first galaxies — small, chaotic, barely-formed clumps of stars from the universe's infancy. Instead, it found massive, fully-formed spiral galaxies at distances where the standard model says the universe was only a few hundred million years old. Cosmologists have been trying to explain this away ever since. I predicted it before the first images came back.

What Was Supposed to Be There

The Big Bang model makes a specific prediction about what the early universe looked like. In the first few hundred million years, the story goes, the universe was full of hot gas and small density fluctuations. Gravity slowly pulled matter together. The first stars lit up. Then small galaxies formed. Then those small galaxies slowly merged over billions of years into the large spiral galaxies we see nearby today.

This means that at extreme distances — at high redshift, in the astronomers' language — you should see only young, small, chaotic, undeveloped galaxies. The Milky Way-sized spirals with their organized structures, their distinct central bulges and sweeping arms, took billions of years to build. You should not see them far away. Far away is long ago, and long ago they did not exist yet.

That was the prediction. The James Webb Space Telescope was going to confirm it.

What Was Actually There

The first deep field images from the James Webb Space Telescope arrived in 2022. Researchers found massive, structured galaxies at redshifts of 7, 10, even 14 — places the standard model places at 300 to 700 million years after the Big Bang. Several of these galaxies were more massive than the Milky Way. Some showed clear spiral structure. Many appeared more organized and mature than anything the standard model predicted at those distances.

The numbers were shocking. The abundance of massive galaxies at high redshift was orders of magnitude higher than standard cosmological models predicted. Not slightly off — orders of magnitude. Multiple papers noted that some of these galaxies appeared to contain more stellar mass than the universe had time to produce, given when they supposedly formed.

The cosmological community responded the way scientific communities typically do: by adjusting the models. Proposals included faster star formation rates, different initial conditions, modified dark matter physics, and revised assumptions about feedback from black holes. Each proposal added new free parameters. None resolved the underlying tension cleanly. The galaxies that should not exist kept appearing.

Why My Framework Has No Paradox

In my framework, there is no paradox — because high redshift does not mean early time.

Redshift is a measure of how much energy a photon has lost on its journey to us. In the standard model, energy is lost because the universe is expanding and stretching the light. In my framework, energy is lost because the light has been traveling a very long distance through the Higgs field. The end result looks the same — the light arrives redder and dimmer than it started — but the interpretation is completely different.

When I observe a galaxy at redshift 10, I am not looking at the universe 13 billion years ago. I am looking at a galaxy that is simply very far away. That galaxy has had just as long to form and mature as any galaxy in the nearby universe. It is not young. It is distant. Those are not the same thing.

In a universe 2,280 billion years old — derived in Article 7 from the recycling cycle timescales — a galaxy at redshift 10 has had billions of years to form. A fully-developed spiral at extreme distance is not a paradox. It is exactly what you would expect.

A Star That Should Not Exist

The James Webb findings are the most dramatic recent example of this tension, but they are not alone. There is a single star in our own galaxy that makes the same point more starkly than any telescope image.

HD 140283 — nicknamed the Methuselah star — is a metal-poor star in the Milky Way. Metal-poor means it formed very early in cosmic history, before generations of stars had time to produce and scatter the heavier elements. Independent measurements of its age converge on approximately 14.46 billion years. The standard model's universe is 13.8 billion years old.

A star cannot be older than the universe it formed in. The standard model has no clean answer to this. My framework does: a 14-billion-year-old star living in a 2,280-billion-year-old universe is unremarkable. It is, in cosmic terms, a young star.

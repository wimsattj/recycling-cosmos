The Universe's Recycling Program

Article 7 (free version) — Joseph Wimsatt, The Recycling Cosmos

Dark matter makes up 27% of everything in the universe. Physicists have been searching for it for decades — with billion-dollar underground detectors, with particle colliders, with telescopes pointed at galaxy clusters. They have found nothing. Not a single confirmed dark matter particle. I think the reason is straightforward: they are looking for the wrong thing. Dark matter is not an exotic undiscovered particle. It is light — ancient light that has lost so much energy it can no longer move.

The Problem That Would Not Go Away

We know dark matter is real. Not because anyone has touched it or measured it directly, but because of what it does. It bends light around galaxy clusters in ways that ordinary matter cannot explain. It holds galaxies together — without it, stars at the edges would fly off into space. It leaves measurable imprints on the large-scale structure of the universe.

Whatever dark matter is, it has mass, it responds to gravity, and it does not interact with light in the way ordinary matter does. The standard model's answer is that dark matter must be a new kind of particle — something produced in the first seconds after the Big Bang, something we have simply not yet found a way to detect.

I find that answer unconvincing. Not because dark matter is not real — it clearly is — but because the standard model has no explanation for why 27% of the universe is dark matter rather than 15% or 50%. In my framework, 27% is not an accident. It is an equilibrium — the balance point of a cycle that has been running for billions of years.

Where Light Goes When It Dies

In previous articles, I described how light loses energy as it travels through the Higgs field — the quantum field that fills all of space and gives particles their mass. The energy loss is tiny over short distances. But over billions of light-years, it adds up. A photon that started its journey in a distant galaxy arrives at Earth slightly dimmer and redder than it left. This is what we observe as cosmological redshift.

Here is the question I asked that led to this framework: what happens to a photon when it has lost nearly all its energy? Where does the energy go?

The answer comes from the physics of the Higgs field itself. There is a threshold energy — set by the same fundamental constants that determine the mass of the electron — below which a photon can no longer exist as light. It cannot propagate freely anymore. Instead, it condenses into a stable, bound state in the Higgs field. It becomes dark matter.

As a photon travels across cosmic distances, it continuously loses energy through interaction with the Higgs field. When the energy drops below a critical threshold, the photon can no longer propagate as light. It condenses into a stable bound state — dark matter.

The Complete Cycle

Once you accept that photons can become dark matter, the rest follows naturally. Dark matter does not stay dark matter forever. Under the extreme conditions inside stellar cores — temperatures in the tens of millions of degrees, pressures beyond anything achievable in a laboratory — the process reverses. Dark matter absorbs energy from the stellar plasma, rises back above the threshold, and reconverts to ordinary matter. To hydrogen.

That hydrogen feeds the next generation of stars. And those stars produce new light. And that light travels across the universe, loses energy, and eventually becomes dark matter again.

Why 27%?

In the standard model, the dark matter fraction — 27% — is whatever it happened to be when the Big Bang produced it. There is no deeper explanation. The number is what it is.

In my framework, 27% is a thermodynamic equilibrium. The rate at which photons are converting to dark matter is exactly balanced by the rate at which dark matter is reconverting to hydrogen inside stars. The universe has been running this cycle long enough — far longer than 13.8 billion years — to have settled into a stable, self-sustaining state.

The implication: The universe is not running down. It is not racing toward heat death. It is a closed recycling loop that has been operating in steady state for approximately 2,280 billion years — over 160 times longer than the standard model's age of the universe. Every atom of hydrogen in your body has been through this cycle. Multiple times.

The Higgs Field — What It Is and Why It Matters

Article 2 (free version) — Joseph Wimsatt, The Recycling Cosmos

In Article 1 we asked: what is light, really? We found that light is a disturbance in the electromagnetic field — a ripple carrying energy across space. Now we ask: what happens to that energy as light travels for billions of years? The answer lies in a field most people have never heard of. And it changes everything about how we read the night sky.

What a Field Actually Is

Before we talk about the Higgs field, we need to understand what a "field" is — because it is one of those words that physicists use as if it is obvious, and it is not.

Imagine the ocean. Every point on the surface has a height — higher where waves push water up, lower in the troughs. That height changes continuously across the entire surface. The ocean is a field: a quantity that exists at every point in space and can change.

Physical fields work the same way. The electromagnetic field exists at every point in space. Where it is disturbed, you get light. Where it is calm, you get empty space. The electron field is another one. The Higgs field is a third.

How fields create particles:

  • Disturb the electromagnetic field → you get a photon (light)
  • Disturb the electron field → you get an electron
  • Disturb the Higgs field → you get a Higgs boson

Fields are not made of anything smaller. They are fundamental. They are the universe.

The Thing That Makes the Higgs Field Unusual

Most fields, at their lowest energy state, have a value of zero. A calm ocean has no height. A field with no disturbances reads zero everywhere.

The Higgs field is different.

Even in a perfect vacuum — no particles, no radiation, no disturbances of any kind — the Higgs field has a non-zero value. Physicists call this the vacuum expectation value. It means the Higgs field is always "on." There is no such thing as a region of space where the Higgs field is absent.

The value is about 246 gigaelectronvolts, a unit of energy density. That may sound abstract, but the consequence is concrete: any particle that moves through space is moving through this non-zero background field. It cannot be switched off or avoided. The Higgs field is everywhere, always.

How Particles Get Mass

This is the famous part — the reason the Higgs boson was called "the God particle" in newspaper headlines, though physicists cringed at the name.

Particles gain mass by interacting with the Higgs field. An electron moving through space is constantly bumping against this non-zero background. That interaction resists its motion, and that resistance is what we call mass. A heavier particle interacts more strongly. A lighter particle interacts less. A photon does not interact at all — which is why light has no mass and always travels at the same speed.

Without the Higgs field, every particle would be massless. There would be no atoms, no chemistry, no stars, no us.

The Part That Has Been Overlooked

Here is where this article goes somewhere most Higgs field explanations do not.

If the Higgs field can give particles mass through interaction, it can also remove energy from particles through the same interaction — given the right conditions.

Photons, as noted, do not interact with the Higgs field at the simplest level of the theory. But at higher orders of quantum field theory, they can. A photon briefly converts to a virtual electron-positron pair, one of which interacts with the Higgs field through a gravitational connection, and the pair converts back to a photon. The photon that comes out has slightly less energy than the one that went in.

The process is extraordinarily subtle. The energy loss per megaparsec is unmeasurable in any laboratory. But over cosmic distances — billions of light-years — the cumulative effect is enormous. Light from distant galaxies arrives at Earth with less energy than it started with. Its wavelength is longer. It is redshifted.

Two explanations produce the same observation:

  • Standard model: Galaxies are rushing away from us. Space is expanding. The wavelength stretches with it.
  • This framework: Galaxies are stationary. Light loses energy as it travels. The wavelength lengthens as energy is lost.

Both produce the same redshift formula. Both fit the data at the current level of observation. The difference is in what follows from each assumption.
